Summary

This paper presents the design, implementation, and results from a dynamic wireless spectrum access system built using GNU Radio and software-defined radios (SDRs) as part of an undergraduate senior design project. The project involved designing and implementing a dynamic wireless spectrum access system in which the secondary user (SU) learns the unknown transmission behavior (channel occupancy and time slots) of the primary user (PU) and then opportunistically transmits during time slots and using channels when they are not being used by the PUs. The main design objective was to maximize the throughput of the SU while minimizing the interference to the PU. A transmitted signal energy detection algorithm with an adaptive threshold was employed to set the channel states as occupied or not occupied. Channel state information was used to determine the PU behavior in a deterministic manner such that the unused time slots and channels could be exploited. A channel allocation scheme for the SU is proposed using the PU channel occupancy information to calculate the channel(s) and time slots available to the SU at any given time. Simulation and physical testing of the system validate the proposed algorithms. Students' feedback affirms GNU Radio and SDRs to be an effective platform for introducing abstract mathematical communications theory concepts, such as cognitive radios and dynamic spectrum allocation, in a hands-on manner.

1 INTRODUCTION

The subject of communications engineering is usually introduced to ECE students in a very abstract theoretical manner. Even though hardware like spectrum analyzers, oscilloscopes, and RF signal generators and simulation tools like MATLAB and Simulink provide some level of hands-on training and visualization of these abstract concepts, however, they are limited in scope and effectiveness. Software-defined radios (SDRs)1-3 and GNU Radio4 provide a powerful platform for designing, implementing, and testing communications engineering concepts. One of the goals of this project was to provide students the ability to design, implement, and test abstract communications engineering concepts using the industry-standard GNU Radio software and HackRF 5 SDRs and gather qualitative efficacy data for the potential inclusion of GNU Radio and SDRs in communications engineering courses.

In recent years, due to an exponential growth in wireless communication devices such as smartphones, wearable wireless devices, tablets, laptops, and embedded wireless sensors, the available wireless spectrum has been shrinking as a multitude of wireless devices fight for the limited wireless spectrum. This was not a problem in the past when the wireless spectrum was not as congested and the Federal Communications Commission (FCC) statically allocated different parts of the wireless spectrum to be used by different organizations and standards. This static allocation of the wireless spectrum is failing to meet the ever-increasing demand for the spectrum due to the ubiquitous penetration of consumer wireless devices. Wireless spectrum, like any resource, is limited and user saturation of the wireless spectrum necessitates an intelligent, cooperative, and dynamic scheme for sharing this limited resource.

A cognitive radio (CR) is a fully reconfigurable wireless communication device that autonomously adapts its communication system parameters to the available spectrum in its environment and the users' demands.6 An SDR is a wireless communication device in which transceiver components that have been traditionally implemented in hardware are instead implemented in software using a PC or embedded system.5, 7 Recent advancements in SDR technology have made fully reconfigurable CR communication systems practical.1-3, 8 For these reasons, we decided to use SDRs to implement the proposed dynamic spectrum allocation scheme.

Building upon the success of CR systems operating in the Wi-Fi (IEEE 802.11x) industrial, scientific, and medical radio (ISM) band, commercial cellular networks have been reconfigured for the implementation of cognitive radio in the current 4G LTE standard and will play a larger role in 5G and prospective standards.

Dynamic spectrum access can enable efficient utilization of the limited wireless spectrum resources.9-12 Measurements performed by the FCC's Spectrum Policy Task Force reveal that at any given time, a significant part of the wireless spectrum is not utilized. Various dynamic spectrum access policies have been explored to better utilize this idle spectrum. Hierarchical access is the most promising solution to efficiently use areas of the spectrum that consist of only white Gaussian noise in time, frequency, and space. Hierarchical access consists of a licensee operating on a legacy system, called primary user (PU), and secondary users (SUs) sharing the same spectrum resources allocated to the PU in a dynamic way. SUs must wait for opportunities to transmit; thus, the term “Opportunistic Spectrum Access (OSA)” is used to describe hierarchical spectrum access. Under this spectrum access model, PUs are not obligated to share their spectrum with SUs; therefore, no modifications to existing PU systems are necessary as severe interference restrictions are placed upon SUs. Furthermore, SUs may have limited to no information about PUs bandwidth, channel allocation, packet length, inter-packet delay, modulation, the physical location of receivers/transmitters, or transmit power. More flexible approaches to this problem are generally sought, and viable solutions to OSA techniques can be complex.

OSA approach consists of two schemes: spectrum underlay and spectrum overlay. Spectrum underlay method allows the SU to transmit a wideband signal that may overlap a PU transmission in time and frequency if certain power restrictions are met. Spectrum overlay method, however, is much less power restrictive as SUs may only access channels during periods of unoccupancy. In many cognitive OSA systems, both methods are used to achieve desirable spectrum efficiency. Opportunities in time and frequency are identified by monitoring spectral characteristics in the overlay approach, and transmission decisions are intelligently determined.

The IEEE International Symposium on Dynamic Spectrum Access Networks (DySPAN) periodically hosts a challenge open to qualified participants to focus on creating highly spectral efficient and environmentally aware radio on existing wireless systems with minimal interference,13-15 This project is a modified version of the DySPAN challenge, with the goal of maximizing spectrum efficiency while monitoring user throughput, aggregated throughput, and interference to the PU.

The paper is organized as follows: In Section 2, we discuss published work related to our research. Section 3 presents a high-level design of the implemented system. Section 4 discusses the modulation and multiplexing scheme used by our system. Sections 5 and 6 present the design and implementation of primary and SUs, respectively. Performance measures and data collected are presented in Section 7. Lastly, we discuss student's reflections and give some future research directions in Section 8.

2 RELATED WORK

Recently, there has been considerable interest in incorporating SDRs into undergraduate electrical engineering education as a means to introduce the mathematically abstract subject of communications engineering.1, 16-22

One very important area of communications engineering, where SDRs can be used as an effective teaching and research tool, is dynamic wireless spectrum allocation using cognitive radios.6, 9-14, 23-25

Even though numerous researchers have explored the use of SDRs in undergraduate electrical engineering education for teaching basic communications engineering concepts,18-21, 26-31 there is a lack of projects exploring more advanced topics of current interest to the wireless communications industry such as cognitive radios and dynamic spectrum allocation at undergraduate level. This paper presents one such project where a dynamic wireless spectrum allocation scheme was implemented using GNU Radio and HackRFs.

3 SYSTEM DESIGN

In the proposed system, the SU and PU dynamically share a 5-MHz frequency band consisting of two 2.5-MHz channels. The lower frequency band is denoted as channel zero (ch0) and the higher band as channel one (ch1). The PU transmitter accesses the channels in a deterministic manner under various transmit scenarios, depicted in Section 5, with no knowledge of SUs or temporal spectrum conditions. The SU system monitors power levels across both channels to determine PU occupancy and ceases transmission upon detection. Received signals from both users are demodulated together and tagged for sorting based upon packet length. Received packets are counted alongside a count of PU attempted transmissions and analyzed for throughput, interference, and spectrum efficiency calculations.

PU and SU systems are completely modeled and tested using GNU Radio software4 and HackRF SDRs.5 GNU Radio flow graphs are used to connect blocks representing various digital signal processing (DSP) elements of the system. GNU Radio core blocks, modified core blocks, and newly developed blocks are used in the system. The DSP code was written in C++11.

4 MODULATION

Data for all users are modulated using quadrature phase-shift keying (QPSK) subcarriers. All of the subcarriers are orthogonally separated in frequency to form orthogonal frequency division multiplexed (OFDM) signals. Two OFDM signals are multiplexed across both channels when applicable.

Quadrature phase shift keying

For modulating the message signals, we used quadrature phase shift keying (QPSK) which offers one of the best tradeoffs between power, bandwidth, and bit error rate. It is widely used in practice such as in Code Division Multiple Access (CDMA) and Digital Video Broadcasting-Satellite (DVB-S). QPSK is a form of digital modulation that has four unique symbols, each transmitting two data bits, where the two data bits per symbol are mapped onto the four distinct phase shifts of a high-frequency carrier pulse. Orthogonal frequency division multiplexing

Orthogonal frequency division multiplexing (OFDM) was used to modulate the multiple QPSK subcarrier signals. OFDM was chosen as the multicarrier modulation scheme because of its various advantages such as immunity to frequency selective fading, resilience to intersymbol interference (ISI), spectrum efficiency, and simpler channel equalizer design. OFDM has been widely adopted by the wireless communications standards and industry including IEEE 802.11 (Wi-Fi), 4G LTE, and 5G.

5 PRIMARY USER

The PU system was modeled from the PU system used in the DySPAN spectrum challenge15 and modified to operate using only two channels. Transmit behavior was adjusted to reflect a two-channel scenario.

Scenarios

Ten different operating scenarios, derived from those in the DySPAN spectrum challenge, were used for the PU. These PU transmit patterns, shown in Table 1, differ in the number of occupied channels, inter-packet delay, and channel hopping pattern.

Table 1. Primary user transmit scenarios
Scenario Description Inter-packet delay
0 Single random channel 5 ms
1 Single random channel 10 ms
2 Two channels, hopping 5 ms
3 Two channels, hopping 10 ms
4 Two channels, synchronous 10 ms
5 Two channels, synchronous 5 ms
6 Two channels, synchronous 2 ms
7 Two channels, Poisson-distributed delay 20 ms (mean)

9 Two channels, Poisson-distributed delay 5 ms (mean)

Scenarios range from a single channel with a deterministic delay to two channels with independent delays sampled from a Poisson distribution. The changing PU behavior increases the difficulty of detection and classification for SUs.

PU transmitter

The PU transmitter modulates data using OFDM from a binary file to be channelized and transmitted periodically based upon the chosen transmit scenario. Two customized blocks were implemented as part of the PU transmitter along with the core GNU Radio blocks. The implemented customized blocks include a packet generator and a packet controller.

The packet controller block, shown in Figure 5, is the brain of the PU. It operates using random engines that determine the inter-packet delay and occupied channels based on the current scenario. Upon start-up, an initial scenario will be chosen, and after which, the appropriate channels and delays will be selected. A command signal will be sent to the packet generator block to acquire data packets and store them in a queue. The incoming OFDM modulated packets are sent out at specific intervals based on the current delay. We use the sample count method for keeping track of time, where zeros are sent out in between packets. In addition, there is a switch time parameter that tells the block how often to switch scenarios.

The packet generator block, shown in Figure 6, receives the command signal from the packet controller and reads a text file to obtain the data to be transmitted. A packet size parameter, in bytes, is used in this block to read the number of bytes per packet from the input file. The number of packets to be read comes from the command signal sent by the packet controller. Once the command is received, the input file will be opened, and the correct number of bytes will be read based on the packet size and number of packets to be read. Packets sent from this block go through an OFDM transmitter block to create the OFDM modulated packets which are then sent to the packet controller block.

The full PU transmitter system, shown in Figure 7, includes two resamplers to adjust the sampling rate and two cosine generators to shift the channels to their respective frequencies.

On the receiver side, shown in the upper right corner of Figure 7, an OFDM demodulation block is used to demodulate the packets, which are then written back to a file.

6 SECONDARY USER

Overlay spectrum access techniques were implemented by an SU in the system. The general idea is to detect the energy in the channels to determine if they are occupied then use that information to transmit in the available channel(s). The SU system, shown in Figure 8, consisted of a few core GNU Radio blocks and two customized blocks implemented as part of this project: an energy detector and a cognitive allocator.

Energy detector

The energy detector takes a vector input with the FFT values of the received signal. Since the OFDM signals generated are not exactly 2.5 MHz, the outer bins of the FFT are used to get samples of the noise in the channel. These values are used to calculate the noise floor and are averaged over time using a single-pole low-pass IIR filter. The noise magnitude is a Rayleigh random variable with a constant mean. The IIR filter gathers information about the noise mean while allowing for a fast transient response. The frequency and transient response of the averaging IIR filter are shown in Figure 9.

The energy detector takes an input parameter that offsets the noise floor in decibels to create a threshold for determining if a channel is occupied. In this study, the threshold value was selected by experimentation to keep the design simple. The threshold value was selected to ensure that the weaker signals, below the threshold, do not set the channel state to be occupied. Once the threshold is set, the magnitude squared of the fast Fourier transform (FFT), also known as the energy spectral density, is computed. Only the middle half of each channel is averaged to compare to the threshold for determining if a channel is occupied. These channel states are then asynchronously sent to the cognitive allocator via a key value message in GNU Radio, as shown in Figure 8. The functionality of the energy detector block is shown in Figure 10.

Cognitive allocator

The cognitive allocator block, shown in Figure 11, takes two inputs: the channel states and the message to be sent. Channel states are received asynchronously and handled immediately to update the SU's transmit pattern. The SU will transmit on both channels if both are available, only one if one is available, or neither in the case that both channels are occupied.

The receiver of the SU is like the PU receiver and uses an OFDM demodulation block to write the received data to a file.

Opportunistic spectrum access was achieved by the SU against acquired PU transmission data. The SU had an estimated response delay of 2 ms with an estimated variance of 1 ms. This could be in part due to poor scheduling by the GNU Radio scheduler. Optimization can be done to the forecaster to reduce and stabilize this delay.

The observed measurements show highly efficient throughput and spectrum usage. Collisions with PUs are visible and unavoidable as this system models an extreme case of SU aggression. Interference was found to be well beyond 5%. Due to the steep learning curve of GNU Radio and HackRF, lack of documentation, and the students' time constraints, extensive performance analysis data could not be collected for this project. However, in future extensions of this project, performance analysis studies will be performed in greater detail. The main goal of this project was the implementation of a noncooperative transmitter energy detection-based spectrum sensing communication system using GNU Radio and SDRs, which the students successfully achieved.

The current implementation of the SU updates the transmit pattern based on the current channel states. This means that there will always be collisions with the PU as well as severe interference. A better approach could involve modeling the PU over a specified interval of time and determining which scenario it is transmitting in. Once the scenario is determined, the SU could decide when and which channels to transmit on, so that it does not collide with the PU. This would greatly reduce the interference caused to the PU as well as improve the overall performance of the system.

8 STUDENTS' REFLECTIONS

The abstract and mathematical nature of the subject of communications engineering poses certain challenges when it comes to teaching this subject to undergraduate electrical engineering students in an effective manner. Modern laboratory equipment such as spectrum analyzers, oscilloscopes, and RF signal generators provide one way of visualizing the abstract communications engineering concepts. However, they do not provide a flexible means of developing a complete communications transmitter and receiver system. Simulation tools such as MATLAB and Simulink, although more flexible in simulating and visualizing a wide variety of communication systems, do not provide exposure to hardware and real signals, a critical skill for the communications engineering job market. SDRs and GNU Radio provide a unique learning platform that offers the best of both worlds: flexibility of building complete communication systems and an ability to work directly with real signals and hardware. A major goal of this project was to leverage the effectiveness of SDRs and GNU Radio in providing undergraduate electrical engineering students an opportunity to design, implement, and test abstract communications engineering concepts. To gather evidence for the attainment of this goal, qualitative data from the students were collected at the end of the project for the potential inclusion of GNU Radio and SDRs in communications engineering courses.

Due to the fact that only two students worked on this project, only qualitative data in the form of interview questions were collected from them. At the end of the project, the project mentor asked for students' feedback with respect to how the project met its goals of providing them hands-on opportunity to implement real-world applications of communications engineering using the latest industry software and hardware platforms.

The students' response to the end-of-project qualitative survey was overwhelmingly positive. The students strongly agreed that the project met its goals of significantly increasing their interest in the area of communications engineering, providing hands-on experience of implementing abstract mathematical communications engineering concepts, and learning industry-standard GNU Radio software and HackRF SDR platforms. It is also noteworthy that one of the students working on this project got a job offer based on his experience with GNU Radio and SDR platforms. The students were of the unanimous opinion that more projects like this should be offered along with the traditional ECE courses to inculcate a stronger interest in communications engineering and making them industry-ready.

9 FUTURE WORK

More complex spectrum awareness methods could be implemented, such as pattern detection through machine learning algorithms conjoined with probabilistic modeling. Real-time environmental awareness could be implemented by machine learning through an artificial neural network (ANN) but would require a listening period. Simpler probabilistic modeling could be achieved during a shorter listening window with more conservative transmit behavior initially, and more aggressive transmissions as the data length provides more confidence in the statistics. SUs could adjust packet size dynamically to achieve even more throughput within the prospected time frame of opportunity.

More channels could be added, or bandwidth could be adjusted for other modulation schemes to add complexity for testing of opportunistic spectrum access solutions. GNU Radio allows up to 22 M samples per second for baseband processing, directly translating to 22 MHz of bandwidth. A transmitter could create multiplexed signals wider than 22 MHz, but a single receiver will not be able to access the full bandwidth of the message signal. Dynamically varying the PU gain during the test would add another layer of complexity.

The addition of the underlay method during long PU bursts would further optimize the spectrum efficiency, SU throughput, and robustness of the system. Additional PU scenarios could be added to complement this method. Careful power considerations would need to be taken into account. A cost-benefit analysis could determine what criteria need to be met to justify the use of underlay method. The underlay method usually involves locating the primary transmitter and receiver to determine appropriate power allocation for minimum interference. This could be kept a constant assumption though, as locations of PUs are known and static during the time of testing.

Beamforming with adaptive MIMO antennae may be implemented to control the spatial distribution of signal power effectively, allowing SUs to transmit to their respective receivers while causing less interference to PUs. This would be especially helpful in conjunction with underlay access method.

In sum, GNU Radio and SDRs provide an effective platform for introducing abstract mathematical communications theory concepts to undergraduate ECE students including the latest wireless technology standards, cognitive radios, dynamic spectrum allocation, and multiple access techniques.
